Sleep, or something will come for you

Lullabies were not born just to soothe children. They also carried the fears of adults.

By Mente Crua Team17 de julho de 202614 min de leitura
Reader available

There is something profoundly strange about trying to put a child to sleep by saying that a creature is approaching to pick it up. Even stranger is to do this with a delicate voice, slow movements and affectionate expression, as if protection and threat were natural parts of the same gesture.

Maybe you're thinking that the baby didn't even understand those words. The baby probably didn't. Thankfully. The baby heard the rhythm, recognized the voice and felt the body of the person rocking the cradle. But those who sang understood perfectly what they were saying.

It is at this moment that the song stops talking only about the child. Entire generations have fallen asleep hearing that the Cuca, the Boogeyman, the Tutu Marambá or the Black-faced Ox They could appear if sleep was delayed.

The melody promised tranquility. The narrative offered obedience as a condition of safety. Perhaps lullabies were never just songs for babies. Perhaps they were also small spaces in which adults deposited tiredness, poverty, fear, loneliness, death, absence and the desperate feeling of caring for someone fragile in a world that was never completely safe.

The music calmed down. The story, not so much

A lullaby produces security by elements that do not depend on the literal understanding of words. Repetition, slow tempo, body contact, and a familiar voice help organize attention and decrease the baby's fuss.

This explains part of the contradiction. The child could be reassured by the form while the adult sang about danger. But perhaps the lyrics were not entirely addressed to the child.

Anyone who has spent a lot of time trying to calm a child knows that singing also organizes those who sing. The breath finds a rhythm, the repetitive gesture gains continuity and the voice occupies the silence. Music regulates two bodies at the same time: that of the child who resists sleep and that of the adult who tries not to fall apart.

It's hard not to wonder how many of these lyrics were actually confessions in disguise. A mother might not have space to say she was exhausted. A caregiver might not be allowed to complain. A family might not have the words to speak of hunger, disease, or mortality. But everyone could sing.

The child heard a melody. The adult heard their own anguish transformed into a story.

Brazil: Sleep protected by fear

In Brazilian songs, danger is usually given a name, a face and an intention. Cuca comes to take the child away. The Boogeyman waits on the roof. The Black-faced Ox appears linked to fear. Tutu Marambá transforms the night into a space watched by something that the child cannot see.

Dorme, menino, que a noite já vem;

fica no colo e não chama ninguém.

Lá fora a criatura percorre o telhado,

mas dentro da casa você está guardado.

When we listen to these songs as adults, the question almost arises on its own: what child would be able to sleep peacefully after understanding all this?

The very young babies probably did not understand the entire narrative. However, as language developed, the monster began to fulfill a clear function. The monster set a boundary: be quiet, don't go out, don't cry, don't disobey, and don't stay awake.

Fear worked as a quick, cheap, and easy-to-transmit educational tool. Before explanations about Child development, sleep routine or Emotional regulation, there were tired adults repeating what they had heard from their own caregivers.

We don't need to turn these people into villains. Many lived surrounded by hard work, insecurity, full houses and little possibility of patiently negotiating with a child. Still, the mechanism deserves to be watched for what it was: an attempt to produce behavior through threat.

The Brazilian tradition was not isolated. Cuca belongs to a family of figures related to the Coco and Coca, present in Portuguese, Spanish and Latin American traditions. The name changed. The message remained recognizable: as long as you obey, you are safe. Out of this obedience, something can find you.

England: a cradle high up in a tree

In Rock-a-bye Baby, one of the best-known lullabies in the English-speaking universe, the baby appears in a cradle on a branch. The wind blows, the branch breaks and the cradle falls.

Inglaterra — versão editorial em português

No alto da árvore o berço balança,

levado pelo vento que sopra no galho.

Quando a madeira se parte na noite,

berço e proteção descem juntos ao chão.

Tradução editorial do motivo narrativo tradicional de Rock-a-bye Baby.

There are different hypotheses about the origin of the song, and many of them are repeated as certainties without sufficient documentation. The element that remains is the unsettling image of a structure made to protect the child supported in an impossible place.

Perhaps the branch is just a poetic resource. But it can also be read as an involuntary representation of the experience of caring. The adult rocks, protects and watches, knowing that he does not control the wind, the disease, the accident or the future.

The cradle represents security, but it is tied to the fragility of the world. The melody says that everything will be fine. The story reminds us that maybe it won't.

Russia: Don't sleep near the edge

In a well-known Russian lullaby, the child is warned not to stay near the edge of the bed. Otherwise, a small grey wolf may appear and carry the child into the forest.

Rússia — versão editorial em português

Dorme longe da borda, pequeno,

não deixes o cobertor escapar.

O lobo cinzento ronda a janela

e conhece o caminho para a mata.

Tradução editorial do motivo tradicional russo associado ao lobo e à borda da cama.

In this case, the threat contains a practical instruction. Do not approach the edge. Do not exceed the protected space. The wolf transforms a domestic boundary into a story impossible to forget.

The bed is no longer just a piece of furniture. Its bank becomes the border between the house and the forest. The room remains safe only as long as the rule is respected.

Does it sound cruel? Maybe it is. But it also reveals a recurring human solution: when an abstract explanation is not enough, a creature is invented. The child may not understand the risk of falling, but understands that meeting the wolf is best avoided.

Japan: when the voice next to the crib was also childish

A Itsuki's Lullaby, originally from the Japanese region of Kumamoto, shifts the focus from the baby to the person rocking the cradle. The song is associated with the lives of poor young caregivers sent to work for other families.

Japão — versão editorial em português

Embalo o filho de outra casa,

enquanto a minha permanece distante.

Minha voz adormece esta criança,

mas não adormece a saudade que carrego.

Tradução editorial dos temas sociais associados à canção de ninar de Itsuki.

No monster is needed here. What scares is reality.

When we imagine a lullaby, we usually see a mother protected inside the house, voluntarily singing to her own child. Many songs, however, were transmitted by nannies, maids, poor girls and women working away from their families.

The baby could be the only listener in front of whom they could speak without being interrupted. The song calmed the child, but also helped the caregiver to endure the repetition of work, distance and inequality.

The affection could be true even when it existed within an unfair relationship. Maybe that makes these songs even more human. They were not born of pure and organized feelings. They were born of people who were able to feel tenderness and resentment at the same time.

Latin America: Sleeping while mom works

In Duerme Negrito, widespread in different Latin American countries, the child is cradled while the mother works in the fields. The song combines the promise of food, maternal absence, exploitation and threat.

América Latina — versão editorial em português

Dorme enquanto tua mãe trabalha,

longe da casa e perto do campo.

Ela voltará trazendo alimento,

se o trabalho não lhe tomar o caminho.

Recriação editorial inspirada nos temas de trabalho, ausência e promessa presentes em Duerme Negrito.

Danger does not come only from the supernatural. It has hierarchy, poverty and social violence. The child needs to sleep not because the house has found peace, but because the adult world keeps moving without being able to stop for the child.

The mother is far away. The caregiver tries to calm down. Future food is promised. The threat appears to complete what tenderness may not be able to accomplish alone.

It is a small social drama sung by a cradle.

Death appeared because it was around

For much of history, losing a baby or a small child was a present possibility. Diseases, hunger, accidents and lack of treatment transformed care into a constant coexistence with the possibility of loss.

It is not surprising that some songs speak of falls, disappearances and funerals. This does not necessarily mean that the adults wished for the child's death. Singing about danger could be an attempt to give shape to something that had no explanation or control.

Chance has no face. The disease cannot be expelled from the roof. Mortality does not obey threats. But the monster may be given a name.

When fear takes shape, it becomes possible to tell a story about it. It is possible to say where it lives, when it appears and what should be done to keep it away. The narrative offers a sense of control, even when true danger remains out of reach.

Perhaps this is why so many cultures have populated the children's room with creatures. Not because adults were unaware of fear, but because they knew it too well.

What does psychology see in this contradiction?

It would be simple to say that all these songs traumatized children. It would also be simple to say that they were just innocent jokes. None of these answers explains the entire phenomenon.

Very young babies respond primarily to rhythm, repetition, intonation, and caregiver presence. An older child, however, already understands words, associates characters with dangers and notices when the threat is used outside the music.

There is a fundamental difference between experiencing fear in safety and being controlled by it. A scary story told on the lap of someone trusted can allow the child to explore difficult emotions without facing real danger.

The adult presents the threat and, at the same time, remains there to cross it together. But when the adult transforms the creature into a permanent authority, the message changes.

"There is something scary, but you are with me" teaches that fear can be faced. "Obey, or it will take you" teaches that protection depends on submission.

Many lullabies live right on this border.

Fear was an education technology

Before there were explanations adapted to childhood, many communities taught limits through stories. Don't go to the forest. Do not approach the river. Do not leave after dark. Do not stand near the edge. Do not disobey.

Abstract danger was transformed into wolf, witch, spirit, Boogeyman or creature from the roof. The story was easy to memorize, could cross generations orally, and worked even when the child did not yet understand complex cause-and-consequence relationships.

Fear quickly solved the problem of behavior, but it hid the real reason for the rule. The child learned to avoid the forest because of the monster, not because he could get lost. He avoided the river because of the creature, not because he might drown.

You may recognize this mechanism even though you didn't grow up listening to the exact same songs. It lives on whenever we invent a threatening presence to achieve immediate obedience.

The monster is efficient. Understanding takes longer.

Every society sings what it cannot forget

In regions surrounded by forests, danger can emerge like a wolf. In Iberian and Latin American traditions, it appears as a creature that kidnaps children. In communities marked by child labor and inequality, music preserves the voice of the exploited caregiver. Where loss was frequent, death enters the lyrics without asking permission.

These songs are emotional maps. They record who cared for, who worked, who was absent, what animals surrounded the community, what dangers existed beyond the home, and what kind of obedience was expected of childhood.

The way to calm down can cross borders. The voice slows down, the melody repeats itself and the body finds a rhythm. But the stories carry local experiences.

The melody can be almost universal. The monster always speaks with an accent.

Maybe the song was never just for the child

When an adult sings to a baby, there seems to be a simple communication: someone cares and someone receives care. But there is another conversation going on.

Repetition calms those who sing. The rhythm organizes the breathing. The melody creates a few minutes of order within a life that is perhaps falling apart. The lyrics allow difficult emotions to appear disguised as tradition.

Maybe a mother couldn't say she was desperate. Perhaps a caregiver could not say that she wanted to leave. Perhaps a father could not admit the fear of losing his child. But everyone could sing.

Lullabies carried affection because there was affection. They carried fear because there was fear. One thing does not eliminate the other. They belong to the same contradictory experience of loving someone deeply and realizing that this love does not offer control over everything that can happen.

So when we hear a gentle voice today talking about monsters, falls, and disappearances, we are not just facing a strange habit from the past. We're hearing adults trying to survive their own nights.

Perhaps the scariest thing is not that they sang terrible things to put a child to sleep. Maybe it's realizing that, in many cases, singing was the only way they had to talk about what didn't let them sleep.

Cuca

Figura do folclore brasileiro associada ao medo noturno, à infância e às histórias usadas para fazer crianças obedecerem ou dormirem.

Origem A Cuca pertence à tradição oral brasileira e se relaciona a antigas figuras ibéricas conhecidas como Coca ou Coco. Ao longo do tempo, recebeu diferentes aparências, nomes e interpretações.
Função cultural Em muitas narrativas, ela aparece como criatura que vigia, ameaça ou leva crianças desobedientes. O monstro transforma regras abstratas em uma imagem fácil de lembrar.
Relação com este artigo Aqui, a Cuca representa o uso do medo como ferramenta de educação: durma, permaneça quieto e obedeça para continuar protegido.
Quando a infância é educada pelo medo, monstros deixam de ser apenas monstros. Eles se transformam em ferramentas culturais.
